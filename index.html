04 The field guide

These aren't unrelated bugs. Read enough of them and they collapse into three kinds of wrong assumption a system makes about text and jurisdiction: the same shapes, repeating across codebases. I wrote it down as a public taxonomy, with a runnable test behind every row.

Format
mismatch
Assumes one character is one byte and one column. Then text is cut mid-kanji, the cursor lands wrong, and two equal strings compare unequal.
6 fixtures · CJK · RTL · combining marks
Legal /
schema
Assumes one global shape for an invoice, an address, a receipt. Then 特商法 and インボイス have nowhere to go, and a validator rejects a valid local value.
the compliance layer · 特商法 · 適格請求書
Cultural /
UX
Assumes every keystroke commits and text flows left to right. Then the IME-confirm Enter is read as submit, and composition is dropped on focus change.
5 fixtures · IME · bidi

Eleven runnable cases live in cjk-agent-fixtures (JS + Go, MIT). Grouping by the broken assumption, not the symptom, tells you the order to fix things and where the next one shows up.

07 Why this, and not a CV

It started as Japanese localization. I'm in Tokyo, and I kept hitting global tools that were almost usable in Japanese but not quite — a missing 特商法 page, a half-translated dashboard, an input box that mangled CJK. Those are Japan-shaped holes: not flaws, just a market most teams structurally can't see. So I filed the fix.

Then I kept reading. Once you're inside a real codebase looking for one kind of gap, you start seeing others — a filter that doesn't clean up after itself, a cookie that corrupts when it's bytes, an underflow that only shows at a narrow terminal. None of those are about Japan. They're just bugs, and the fix ships the same way: a diff a maintainer merges.

I don't have a name attached to this, and I'd rather you didn't take my word for any of it. Everything here points at a third party that can't be edited by me — a merged PR, an App Store listing, a public dataset. If a claim doesn't link out, I shouldn't have made it. That's the whole bet: proof you can check beats a story you have to trust.
